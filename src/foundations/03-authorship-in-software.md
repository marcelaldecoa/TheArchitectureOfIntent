# Pattern 1.3 — Authorship in Software

**Part I: Foundations** · *Pattern 3 of 6*

---

> *"A system still expresses someone's will. Authorship does not disappear — it moves upstream."*

---

## Context

You are orchestrating agents that produce real systems used by real people. The question of **who is responsible** for these systems — legally, ethically, professionally — is not abstract. It is the question that must be answered before you can delegate anything safely.

This pattern assumes [From Translation to Orchestration](02-from-translation-to-orchestration.md) and prepares the ground for [Where Agency Resides](04-where-agency-resides.md).

---

## The Problem

In the old model, authorship was clear. A developer wrote the code. If the code was wrong, the author was traceable — not always legally, but intellectually. The decision trail went: *someone decided to write this line.* Even when the decision was wrong, the structure of responsibility was legible.

In an agent-mediated model, this trace becomes murkier. The specification was written by a human. The code was generated by an agent. The agent was configured by a platform team. The platform runs on a foundation model trained on billions of documents. The test that failed to catch the bug was written by still another agent.

When the system causes harm, who authored that harm?

---

## The Resolution

Authorship in software has always been about something deeper than keystroke ownership. To author a system is to **originate the intent that the system expresses and to be accountable for its consequences**.

This is not a new idea. Architects are not absolved of responsibility because contractors built their designs. Directors are not absolved of responsibility because actors delivered their scripts. **The human who holds intent holds authorship.**

What has changed is that the distance between intent and consequence has expanded dramatically. You can author a specification that causes a system to make thousands of decisions per second affecting millions of people — and never touch a line of code.

That expansion of distance is an expansion of **moral exposure**. The more powerful the delegation, the more significant the authorship.

This has four practical implications:

### 1. Intent Is the Author's Fingerprint

Every specification is an expression of what the author understood, valued, and decided. Ambiguities in the spec are not neutral — they express what the author failed to think through. Constraints in the spec express what the author knew must be protected.

If the spec said nothing about data privacy, and the agent violated it, who is the author of that violation?

### 2. Constraints Are Moral Commitments

When you write a constraint — *this system must never expose user data to unauthenticated callers* — you are making a commitment on behalf of the users of that system. Constraints are not technical restrictions; they are **ethical commitments encoded in advance**.

This is why [Why Specs Are Moral Artifacts](../theory/06-why-specs-are-moral-artifacts.md) is a theory-level chapter, not a methodology chapter. It addresses what the spec *is*, not just how to write one.

### 3. The Oversight Function Is Irreducible

If a human author is responsible for what a system does, then that human must have the capacity to understand what the system does and to correct it when it diverges from intent.

This is why [Human Oversight Models](../agents/06-human-oversight-models.md) is not optional. Oversight is not a governance formality — it is the mechanism by which authorship remains meaningful. An author who cannot see what their creation is doing has not delegated; they have abandoned.

### 4. Authorship Does Not Require Proximity

A specification written six months ago by an engineer who has since left the team is still an expression of authorship. The author is accountable for what the spec said. This has implications for [Living Specs and Feedback Loops](../sdd/06-living-specs.md) — specs cannot be abandoned any more than software can be abandoned.

---

## Therefore

> **To author a software system is to originate the intent it expresses and to be accountable for its consequences. When agents execute on specifications, the authors of the specifications are the authors of the outcomes. Authorship does not disappear with delegation — it becomes more concentrated and more consequential.**
